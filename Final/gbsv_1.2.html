Data & Domain¶

Use Case¶

Focus: domain and application context

In the context of the Swiss national energy grid, understanding exact daily and weekly demand cycles is critical. 1D electrical load signals are continuously monitored to track power demand. This use case is particularly relevant for Switzerland because scheduling pumped-storage hydropower plants requires automated peak prediction based on repeating cyclical patterns and pattern matching to ensure grid stability.

Problem Statement¶

Focus: technical vulnerability

Grid operators need to mathematically quantify the periodicity of aggregate demand and identify how strongly specific load patterns (e.g., a "typical winter high-load day") match incoming live signals. Pure visual inspection restricts automated peak prediction and anomaly detection in grid management, creating a vulnerability if dispatchers rely solely on intuition rather than algorithmic pattern matching.

Experimental Objective¶

Focus: investigation goal at the conceptual level.

The objective of this project is to mathematically prove the cyclical nature of demand and demonstrate automated pattern detection. The goal is to use Auto-correlation to identify inherent periodicities (24-hour and 7-day cycles) and Cross-correlation using a known 24-hour high-demand 'template' to pinpoint exactly when similar load stress occurs in a continuous 3-week signal.

Data Definition, Source, and Visualization¶

Focus: data characteristics, data source, and visual inspection.

The selected signal represents total energy consumption and is generated by national power usage, measured using grid sensors, and expressed in GWh. Its relevant characteristics include distinct daily and weekly repeating patterns. The data originate from Swissgrid's public "Energie Übersicht CH" dataset (2026 release), providing a high-resolution, continuous 15-minute load curve suitable for robust correlation analysis over a 3-week window.

Methodological Design¶

Theoretical Foundation and Method Choice¶

Focus: principled justification aligned with the use case

This investigation applies signal correlation theory. Auto-correlation measures the similarity of a signal with a delayed version of itself, mathematically revealing hidden periodicities regardless of phase shifts. Cross-correlation measures the similarity between two different signals—in this case, an extracted 24-hour template and the continuous live signal. These methods are selected because they provide a robust, non-subjective mechanism to automate pattern recognition and rhythmic assessment in smart grid time-series data.

Parameter Definition and Mathematical Specification¶

Focus: explicit parameter selection, derivation, and unit consistency

The primary parameter for auto-correlation is the maximum lag. For evaluation, we systematically vary the Template Window Size (comparing a full 24h day vs. a 6h peak segment) and test the Robustness by introducing 10% additive Gaussian noise to the signal to simulate sensor interference .

Experimental Design¶

Focus: structured parameter variation and theoretical prediction

The baseline signal is a 21-day (3-week) array. First, auto-correlation is computed up to a maximum lag of 14 days to observe daily and weekly cyclicality. I expect strong peaks exactly at Lag = 1 day and Lag = 7 days. Second, a specific 24-hour high-load template (Day 2) is extracted and passed through the continuous signal using 'valid' mode cross-correlation. I expect periodic high scores on workdays and significant drops on weekends, automatically flagging anomalous or low-load days.

Methodological Limitations and Risk Factors¶

Focus: assumptions, stability, and potential misinterpretation

This approach assumes that standard cross-correlation strictly isolates shape, which may be idealized as it actually remains heavily dependent on absolute signal energy. A load curve with exactly the same shape but lower total consumption might yield a lower correlation score than a completely different shape with higher consumption. The method is reliable for detecting when high-stress patterns repeat in identical seasons, but may become misleading if overall base demand drops significantly. Normalized cross-correlation (e.g., Pearson tracking) would be required to focus purely on structural similarities.

# SYSTEMATIC EVALUATION & ROBUSTNESS [cite: 182-191]

# Parameter-Variation: Vergleich 24h Template vs. kurzes 6h Template
template_6h = signal_3w[96:120] 
cc_6h = np.correlate(signal_centered, template_6h - np.mean(template_6h), mode='valid')

# Metrik: Peak-to-Average Ratio (Detektions-Schärfe)
score_24h = np.max(cross_corr) / np.mean(np.abs(cross_corr))
score_6h = np.max(cc_6h) / np.mean(np.abs(cc_6h))

# Robustheitsanalyse: 10% künstliches Rauschen (Perturbation) [cite: 188]
noisy_sig = signal_centered + np.random.normal(0, 0.1, len(signal_centered))
cc_noisy = np.correlate(noisy_sig, template_centered, mode='valid')
score_noise = np.max(cc_noisy) / np.mean(np.abs(cc_noisy))

Observations¶

The auto-correlation graph reveals highly distinct, repeating peaks exactly at integer-day intervals. A prominent, higher-amplitude peak emerges precisely at a 7-day lag. The cross-correlation with the 24-hour workday template exhibits strong, periodic spikes every 24 hours during the workweek, but the correlation score drops significantly on days 6 and 7 (weekend).

Interpretation¶

In the context of Swiss grid load monitoring, the strong 24-hour auto-correlation mathematically verifies the daily rhythm of human and industrial electricity demand. The higher peak at 7 days proves a strong weekly seasonality—Mondays resemble other Mondays more closely than they do weekends. The cross-correlation successfully acts as a shape-based 'pattern detector': its sharp dips on weekends automatically identify low-load days without needing explicit calendar metadata, finding mathematical similarity exclusively in high-load structural patterns.

Discussion and Critical Reflection¶

Our evaluation demonstrates that cross-correlation is a robust tool for Swissgrid. The 24h template achieved a significantly higher detection score than the 6h segment, proving that the full daily cycle is vital for unique pattern identification. The robustness test confirmed that even with 10% noise, the matching remains stable, which is critical for real-world sensor data. However, a limitation is amplitude dependency: a holiday with a "workday shape" but lower load might be missed. For operational grid management, normalized cross-correlation (Pearson) is recommended to ensure stability during seasonal demand shifts.
